## HEART TALK #67

### AN UPDATE ON HEART AND INFLAMMATION
Inflammation is a normal part of the body’s defense to injury or infection, and in this way, it is beneficial. But inflammation is damaging when it occurs in healthy tissues or lasts longer when it  is known as chronic inflammation. It may persist for months or years.

During the past three decades or so, research has shown that inflammation is as important as cholesterol in the development of atherosclerosis. At present, it is recommended that screening for inflammation, which is easily measured with an inexpensive blood test be done along with your lipid screening.

### DOES INFLAMMATION IS A FRIEND OR ENEMY
Inflammation  comes in two forms, acute, which is short lived and would often cause symptoms, such as pain or swelling, or it can be chronic, which is long lasting and often silent. Acute inflammation is a natural immune response to a health threat, such as injury or infection and results in an outpouring of white blood cells and other chemical messengers which help to heal the wound or eliminate the bug causing the infection. On the other hand, chronic low-grade inflammation often initiated by harmful substances, such as high blood sugar or excessive fat in the tissues. This slow chronic process has become increasingly common in our current environment and it is common factor in many health conditions, including
cardiovascular disease .

If someone has only a small amount of plaque inside their arteries supplying the heart or the brain, our immune system treats that plaque like any other invader. Immune cells come in to fight the insult which in turn covers the plaque with a fibrous cap. However, the inflammation underneath can persists and can cause the cap to rupture. When  the plaque ruptures, the debris inside the plaque can go into the bloodstream where it can cause blood clot , causing heart attack or stroke, depending upon which artery is involved.

About 50% of the heart attack victims have risk factors such as smoking, high blood pressure, high cholesterol and diabetes. Other 50% of the heart attack victims have none of these major risk factors and inflammation  has long been suspected as a possible culprit. At present there  is clear evidence that elevated markers of inflammation to heart disease and treating inflammation can prevent future risk of heart attacks or strokes. 

American College of cardiology and American Heart Association recently published cholesterol guidelines which clearly states that high sensitivity test for C- reactive protein, called  hs CRP be measured along with your lipid screening(  and also ApoB testing and Lipoprorein (a) )     A hs CRP level less than 1 mg/L suggests that you are at risk is low, 1 to 3 mg/L means average risk and more than 3 mg/L is high risk for cardiovascular disease and stroke. The present recommendation calls for CRP testing along with lipid panel  in age 30s and 40s so that we intervene earlier in life. Checking the CRP level is particularly important among women, since cardiovascular disease remains under diagnosed and under treated in this group. People with autoimmune disorders like rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, HIV should also monitor CRP level since their immune systems are already impaired.

WHAT CAN BE DONE
It is well known that lifestyle changes can help us,as also statin drugs. Statin drugs, not only lower the cholesterol level,  they decrease the inflammation, and reduce the risk of plaque rupture. Anyone with a persistently high hs CRP level should consider talking to their healthcare provider about taking statin if they’re not already on it or if they are already taking one, switching to a higher dose. 
Another option for patients who have heart disease and taking high intensity startins, but continue to have high hs CRP, greater than 2 mg/L could be a candidate for low dose Colchcine, 0.5 milligram daily. This drug used in the treatment of gout has been approved for anti-inflammatory therapy by FDA. This drug when added to statin therapy has proven to reduce heart disease risk.

WAYS TO REDUCE INFLAMMATION
Most of the healthy habits that lower your risk of heart disease and other chronic conditions can help to reduce inflammation. These include.
-Mediterranean or DASH diet, which encourages foods that fight inflammation, including fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and olive oil
-avoid foods that cause inflammation, which include sodas and other sugar laden  drinks, red and processed meats and refined carbohydrates-especially white bread and snacks or sweets, made with white flour
-Regular exercise-at least 150 minutes of moderate intensity exercise or  75 minutes of vigorous exercise per week.
-Avoid smoking
-for non-vegetarians, eating 2 to 3 servings of fish per week, which include salmon, mackerel, herring, which are rich inin omega-3 fatty acids. 
 
As always, make sure you check with your healthcare providers before you follow any of the above suggestions
