### HEART TALK #66

## UPDATED GUIDELINE FOR-MANAGING ABNORMAL LIPIDS

Studies have shown that 80% or more of the cardiovascular disease is preventable and an elevated LDL cholesterol, often called bad cholesterol is a major part of the risk. Optimizing healthy lifestyle habits such as regular physical exercise, heart-healthy eating, not smoking, maintaining an ideal body weight and adequate good quality sleep are the first steps to lower cholesterol. If lipid numbers are not within the desirable range after a period of lifestyle optimization, adding lipid lowering medications earlier than previously thought makes sense. Lower cholesterol for longer, just like lower blood pressure for longer,  results in much greater protection agonist future heart attack and stroke risk.

American College of Cardiology and American Heart Association announced their new cholesterol guidelines last week. If you had read my previous heart talks, I have mentioned many of these. 

Here is the update:
	1.	USE THE NEWER PREVENT EQUATION TO CALCULATE THE RISK. PREVENT EQUATION ( Predicting Risk of cardiovascular disease EVENTs) was developed in 2023. This new equation helped to predict the cardiovascular risk for both 10 years and 30 years and helps to guide lipid lowering medication in primary prevention in adults between the ages of 30 and 79 years. Primary prevention means prevention of cardiovascular disease before an event such as heart attack, stroke, stents and bypass surgery, etc occurs. On the basis of PREVENT EQUATION, 10 year risk is being categorized as follows.
       Less than 3%=Low Risk
      3 to 5%= borderline risk
      5 to 10%= intermediate risk
       More than 10%=High Risk
     PREVENT EQUATION IS AVAILABLE IN THE WEB FOR YOU TO CALCULATE YOUR 10 year and 30 year risk of develping cardiovascular risk 

	2.	Treat elevated cholesterol earlier to reduce lifelong risk of prolonged exposure to bad cholesterol. Lifestyle optimization should start in youth with familial hypercholesterolemia and in young adulthood in people with LDL cholesterol more than 160 mg/dL or a strong family history of premature cardiovascular disease.

3.LDL lowering therapy CAN BE CONSIDERED in adults for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease with a 10 year PREVENT risk estimate of 3 to 5% ( borderline risk) and SHOULD BE CONSIDERED for those at 5 to 10% (Intermediate risk) 10 year risk

	4.	Apolipoprotein B (ApoB) testing can be useful to improve risk assessment and guide treatment, once LDL cholesterol and non-HDL cholesterol goals are met, particularly in those with elevated triglycerides more than 200 mg/dL and diabetes. ApoB is another Atherogenic Lipoprotein and just like LDL cholesterol, also contributes to plaque build up. 
This test may be useful in someone with relatively normal LDL cholesterol and still develops cardiovascular disease. This test may help to identify adults with residual elevated cardiovascular risk that may be underestimated by the standard lipid profile alone.

5.Lipoprotein (a) or Lp(a) should be measured at least once to identify those individuals at higher risk of cardiovascular disease. Lp(a) increases the risk at  levels greater than 50 mg/dL which is associated with about 1.4 fold increased cardiovascular risk and values higher than 100 mg/dL are associated with more than two fold higher risk. The presence of elevated Lp(a) should be an indication for more intensified LDL cholesterol lowering .Since Lp(a) is genetically determined, it needs to be measured only once in lifetime and doesn’t need to be repeated. 

	6.	Selective use of non-contrast coronary artery calcium scan ( CAC scan ). This scan can be used to check for early or sub clinical calcium and plaque buildup in the walls of the coronary arteries. It is recommended for men age 40 and older,and women age 45 and older with borderline or intermediate 10 year risk of heart attack or stroke. Having any amount of calcium in the coronary arteries is abnormal and  needs an LDL cholesterol goal of less than 100 milligram per deciliter- with lower LDL cholesterol target level with higher amount of calcium. 

	7.	Just like LDL cholesterol, non-HDL cholesterol also needs to be at target, which is 30 mg more than LDL target. In other words, if your LDL target is 100, your non-HDL should be less than 130.

	8.	With  the new risk tool, we can better estimate cardiovascular risk, using health information already obtained during an annual physical, total cholesterol, HDL cholesterol blood pressure reading and other personal information, such as age, gender and health habits- and then further personalize the risk score for each  individual by looking at “RISK ENHANCERS” which can help to guide the need for lipid lowering medication
This risk enhancers include a family history of premature heart  disease, chronic inflammatory conditions, ( such as rheumatoid arthritis, lupus), and conditions, such as overweight/obesity, diabetes, chronic kidney disease, higher risk ancestry, such as south east Asians, like Indians or Filipinos. Female specific risk enhancers include early menopause before the age of 40, preeclampsia and diabetes during pregnancy.( gestational Diabetes). Additional markers include Lipoprotein(a). Apolipoprotein B ( ApoB), high sensitivity C reactive protein (hsCRP) or elevated triglycerides

Having healthy, LDL cholesterol levels or high density Lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL or good cholesterol), is not necessarily a “get out of jail free” card. Measuring other bio markers can give a more complete picture of someone’s cardiovascular risk and help  decision about whether lipid loverinng medication is needed sooner rather than later or if more intensive therapy is warranted

The guideline recommends an LDL cholesterol of less than 100 mg/dL for those at borderline or intermediate risk and less than 70 mg/dL in those at higher risk. For individuals with known cardiovascular disease and also people who are at very high risk of heart attack or stroke, the LDL cholesterol goal should be less than 55 mg/dL for secondary prevention of cardiac events
